Roasting Pathways: Home, Garage, Cafe, or Production

The same roasting principles apply at every scale, but the risk, evidence, equipment, and responsibility change. Use this page after the core lessons to decide where you are actually ready to go next.

First, pass the common standard

Before choosing a pathway, complete at least three roast cycles where you can show the full loop: roast plan, roast log, cupping notes, brewing result, and one justified next change. A beautiful curve without taste evidence is not enough.

  • You can explain your green coffee, batch size, heat plan, and flavor goal in plain language.
  • You can identify at least one defect risk before roasting and one sensory clue after roasting.
  • You can repeat a roast closely enough that the cup remains recognizably similar.
  • You can decide when not to roast because ventilation, cleaning, equipment, or fire safety is not ready.

Path 1

Home Roaster

Best for learners who want better coffee at home, small experiments, origin curiosity, and a low-cost way to understand roasting behavior.

Minimum readiness

  • You understand first crack, roast level, rest, and basic defect signs.
  • You can roast safely outdoors or with suitable ventilation.
  • You accept that small machines may behave unevenly and need patient repetition.

Path 2

Garage or Micro-Roastery

Best for someone testing small sales, community orders, farm samples, direct producer lots, or a future roastery idea without jumping too fast.

Minimum readiness

  • You keep batch records and can repeat a profile intentionally.
  • You have safe ventilation, cleaning routines, chaff control, and cooling capacity.
  • You cup every production batch and record customer or brewing feedback.

Path 3

Cafe Roaster

Best for cafes that want coffee roasted for their own bar, with direct control over freshness, brew recipes, espresso behavior, and customer feedback.

Minimum readiness

  • You can translate roast decisions into espresso, filter, milk, and black coffee performance.
  • You can keep one house profile stable while still testing new lots separately.
  • You have a simple quality control routine before coffee reaches customers.

Focus next

  • 1.8 - Matching Roast to Brew Method

Path 4

Production or Factory Roaster

Best for wholesale, larger batch production, repeatable blends, multiple operators, packaged coffee, and coffees that must remain consistent across weeks or months.

Minimum readiness

  • You have written profiles, acceptance limits, batch release checks, and traceable records.
  • You can separate profile development, production roasting, quality control, storage, and dispatch.
  • You understand that safety, maintenance, staff training, and documentation are part of coffee quality.

A simple final assignment

Choose one pathway and write a one-page plan. Include your roaster type, batch size, green coffee, target drink style, safety setup, roast log fields, cupping method, brewing test, packaging/rest plan, and the rule you will use to decide whether the roast is good enough to share.

This is not paperwork for its own sake. It is how a learner becomes a responsible roaster.

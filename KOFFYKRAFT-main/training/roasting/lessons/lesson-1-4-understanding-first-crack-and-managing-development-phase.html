Foundations / Lesson 1.4

Understanding First Crack & Managing Development Phase

The roasting learning loop

  1. Intend: name the coffee, brew use, and flavor goal before heating the machine.
  2. Plan: choose batch size, charge approach, airflow, milestones, and one variable to watch.
  3. Roast and log: record time, temperature, colour, aroma, sound, airflow, gas or power, and decisions.
  4. Cup: evaluate with the Cupping course discipline before defending the profile.
  5. Brew: test with a realistic brew method from the Brewing course.
  6. Revise: change one thing in the next roast and write why.
What this lesson teaches

First Crack is a practical marker, not a magic chemical switch. It helps the roaster manage the final stage toward sweetness, acidity, body, and balance.

How this builds the student

This lesson builds timing discipline and sensory listening.

Cupping connection

Use aroma, sweetness, acidity, body, aftertaste, balance, defects, and confidence notes from the Cupping course. A roast decision is not proven until the cup supports it.

Brewing connection

Check whether the roast extracts sensibly in a real recipe. Sour, hollow, bitter, harsh, or muddy brews may point back to roast development, solubility, or brew setup.

Lesson Objective

By the end of this lesson, you will:

Know exactly what First Crack is - chemically, physically, and practically.

Learn how to detect First Crack, even if it's faint.

Understand how to continue roasting after First Crack based on observed cues.

See how Development Phase shapes flavor, sweetness, and body.

Be introduced to different learning paths for mastering this phase.

New Terms and Concepts

TermMeaning
First CrackAudible popping during roasting as internal bean pressure ruptures the cell walls - marking the start of Development Phase.
Development PhaseThe final roasting stage where caramelization, sweetness, body, and aroma mature.
DTR (Development Time Ratio)The percentage of total roast time spent after First Crack.
UnderdevelopmentEnding roast too soon after crack - resulting in grassy, sour, or hollow flavors.
Baked CoffeeOverextending roast after crack without sufficient momentum - leading to dull, cardboard flavors.
Thermal MomentumThe ongoing internal heat within the bean, which continues to rise even after external heat adjustments.
First Crack MarkingThe act of noting when First Crack begins to guide subsequent roasting stages.

Main Lesson Content

A. Why First Crack Matters

First Crack is a pivotal point:

Ends the Maillard (browning) stage.

Begins the Development Phase.

Gives the roaster active control over the final flavor of the coffee.

Good control from First Crack onward leads to:

Better sweetness,

Balanced acidity,

Fuller body,

Controlled bitterness.

B. What Causes First Crack?

Inside the bean, water vapor and carbon dioxide build pressure as heat increases.

At a critical point (typically 195 deg C-205 deg C internal bean temperature), the cell walls rupture.

This causes the popping noise known as First Crack.

Simultaneously, physical expansion and major chemical transformations begin.

Bean TypeTypical Crack Behavior
High-density beans (e.g., Ethiopian, Kenyan)Crack later and louder.
Low-density beans (e.g., Brazilian Naturals, Monsooned Malabar)Crack earlier; may sound softer.

C. How to Detect First Crack (Even if It's Faint)

Not all roasters produce loud cracks.

You can use multiple sensory markers:

ClueWhat You Notice
SoundCrisp, sharp pops (even if faint) like popping popcorn or crinkling paper.
SmellAroma shift from cereal/grassy to bread, toast, caramel.
ColorBeans move from tan -> light brown.
ExpansionBeans visibly swell; center line may open slightly.
Steam/VaporIn closed systems, a rush of sweet-smelling vapor may accompany cracking.

Important:

Even if First Crack is not clearly audible, consistent marking based on combined signs helps you roast more reliably.

D. Development Phase: What Happens After First Crack

Development is where the coffee's flavor is built:

Sugars caramelize further.

Acidity smooths out.

Body and sweetness increase.

Oils may start migrating to the surface (especially in darker roasts).

Roast LevelTarget DTR (%)Example (for 10-minute total roast)
Light Roast15-20%1.5-2.0 minutes post-crack
Medium Roast20-25%2.0-2.5 minutes post-crack
Dark Roast>25%2.5-3+ minutes post-crack

The balance between acidity, sweetness, body, and bitterness is primarily set during this stage.

E. Two Learning Paths for Understanding Development

Different roasters and schools of thought suggest two broad learning paths:

Learning PathFocusCommon Practices
Structured ApproachCareful measurement and numerical control.Mark exact First Crack time, calculate Development Time Ratio (DTR), track Rate of Rise (RoR), and aim for reproducibility through consistent data.
Sensory ApproachDeveloping intuitive understanding through senses.Observe bean color, smell, and audible crack behavior; adjust heat and airflow naturally based on feedback from the coffee itself, without rigid time or percentage targets.

Both approaches are valid and useful:

Some learners prefer objective measurements early.

Others prefer building sensory memory first, then refining with data.

You are free to explore and blend both paths to create a roasting style that suits your goals, equipment, and personality.

Roasting is a dialogue between structure and feeling - both are tools you can master.

G. How to Progress After Marking First Crack

IssueCauseAction
Roast races too fastExcess heat after crackGradually lower heat, extend development slightly
Roast stalls, smells flatHeat drop too suddenMaintain gentle energy, avoid crashing
Unsure if crack startedTrust combined smell, color, and sound signs

After First Crack, thermal momentum continues to push roast development.

Your role becomes guiding the rate of progress, not over-correcting.

Key Takeaways

First Crack is a major milestone - but it's just the beginning of active roast control.

Development Time shapes sweetness, body, and flavor balance.

Both structured and sensory methods can lead to excellent roasting.

Consistency in marking and reacting is more important than chasing the perfect instant.

Optional Practical Exercise

If roasting:

Start a stopwatch at bean loading.

Note color, aroma, and sound changes.

Mark First Crack time consistently based on sensory confirmation.

Calculate DTR % afterward and taste the results.

If not roasting yet:

Watch public roasting demonstrations from more than one roaster, and focus on what you can hear, smell, and observe rather than copying one operator's settings.

Practice spotting sensory shifts into First Crack.

Reflection Prompt

If your roaster or environment makes First Crack hard to hear, what other signs can you trust?

How might you combine both "structured" and "sensory" paths for your learning journey?

End of Lesson 1.4

Practice assignment

  1. State the roast intention in one sentence.
  2. Roast, observe, or study a roast video with the lesson idea in mind.
  3. Cup the result or a comparable coffee using a simple written form.
  4. Brew the same coffee with one normal method and compare it to the cupping result.
  5. Write one next-roast change and one reason not to change anything else.

Common beginner mistakes

  • Trusting the curve before tasting the coffee.
  • Changing charge, airflow, batch size, and development all at once.
  • Using one roast defect word as a verdict without checking green coffee, machine, log, and cup.
  • Ignoring brew behavior after a roast cups well.

Self-check with answer guide

1. What should this lesson change in your roasting judgment?

Answer: It should give you one clearer observation or decision rule, not a memorized number.

2. How do cupping and brewing protect you from false confidence?

Answer: Cupping isolates roast and green coffee signals; brewing tests whether the roast works in the way someone will actually drink it.

3. What is the safest next experiment?

Answer: Repeat the roast with one planned change, keep the same evaluation method, and compare notes side by side.

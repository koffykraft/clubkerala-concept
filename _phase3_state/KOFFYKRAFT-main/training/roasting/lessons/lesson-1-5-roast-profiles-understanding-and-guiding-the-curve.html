Roast Profiles: Understanding and Guiding the Curve

The roasting learning loop

  1. Intend: name the coffee, brew use, and flavor goal before heating the machine.
  2. Plan: choose batch size, charge approach, airflow, milestones, and one variable to watch.
  3. Roast and log: record time, temperature, colour, aroma, sound, airflow, gas or power, and decisions.
  4. Cup: evaluate with the Cupping course discipline before defending the profile.
  5. Brew: test with a realistic brew method from the Brewing course.
  6. Revise: change one thing in the next roast and write why.
What this lesson teaches

A roast profile is a record of decisions and machine response. It is useful only when interpreted with taste, appearance, and repeatability.

How this builds the student

This lesson builds curve literacy without curve worship.

Cupping connection

Use aroma, sweetness, acidity, body, aftertaste, balance, defects, and confidence notes from the Cupping course. A roast decision is not proven until the cup supports it.

Brewing connection

Check whether the roast extracts sensibly in a real recipe. Sour, hollow, bitter, harsh, or muddy brews may point back to roast development, solubility, or brew setup.

Main Lesson Content

A. What Is a Roast Profile?

A roast profile is a graph plotting:

X-axis = Time (minutes),

Y-axis = Bean Temperature ( deg C).

It visualizes how the beans' temperature changes as roasting progresses.

The profile usually includes:

Charge (beans dropped into roaster),

Turning Point (temperature bottom-out then rise),

Dry End (Yellowing),

Maillard Start (browning begins),

First Crack,

Development Phase,

Drop (End of Roast).

B. Key Stages on a Typical Roast Curve

Time PointWhat HappensTypical Temperature
ChargeBeans loaded into roaster180-210 deg C environment
Turning PointBean temp dips then rises~1-1.5 minutes
Dry End (Yellowing)Moisture mostly evaporated~3-5 minutes
Maillard StartColor and aroma changes~5-6 minutes
First CrackAudible pops~8-9 minutes
Development PhaseSweetness and body build~8.5-12 minutes
Drop/EndRoast completed195-225 deg C bean temp

C. Understanding Rate of Rise (RoR)

Rate of Rise (RoR) measures how fast the bean temperature is climbing:

Units: deg C per minute.

Analogy:

Think of it like a car's speedometer - not the distance you've traveled (temperature), but how fast you're moving right now (RoR).

Why RoR matters:

RoR BehaviorRisk
Too FastScorching, tipping, uneven inside
Too SlowBaking, muted flavors, loss of sweetness

A gentle, steadily declining RoR (a smooth "ski slope" downward) is often considered ideal for balanced roasts.

D. How Curve Shape Influences Flavor

Curve BehaviorTypical Flavor Outcome
Spiky RoR (erratic jumps)Inconsistent, sharp or bitter notes
Flat RoR (little rise)Underdeveloped, grassy, sour flavors
Declining RoR (gentle slope)Even roasting, balanced sweetness and body
Very Slow FinishBaked, muted, lifeless cup
Very Fast FinishOutside burnt, inside undercooked

E. Two Ways Roasters Approach Curve Control

There are different philosophies when it comes to controlling roast curves:

ApproachCharacteristicsFocus
Structured Curve ManagementTarget smooth declining RoR; track every phase numerically; adjust airflow, gas, drum speed.High reproducibility; quantitative precision.
Sensory-Guided RoastingLet bean feedback (color, smell, crack sound) guide adjustments; accept natural curve variations.Sensory learning; flexible, intuitive roasting based on real-time observation.

Both approaches can lead to excellent cups:

Some roasters find security in strict data control.

Others embrace small curve variations if flavor outcomes are good.

You are encouraged to learn from both and discover your own style.

There are many paths to a beautiful roast - and the final cup, not the graph, is the real goal.

Even without a roaster:

Watching sample roast curves helps you understand how different choices affect the bean's journey.

Key Takeaways

Roast profiles tell the story of the bean's journey from green to roasted.

Rate of Rise (RoR) is the "speed" of roasting - smooth control helps flavor development.

Different roasters interpret curves differently - some prioritize strict curve shapes; others prioritize cup quality.

Consistent, aware, and thoughtful roasting leads to the best outcomes, whatever path you follow.

Dry End

Reflection Prompt

If you had two roasts - one with a perfect smooth curve but a flat taste, and another with a slightly messy curve but a beautiful cup - which would you prefer?

What does this teach about balancing data and sensory outcomes?

End of Lesson 1.5

Practice assignment

  1. State the roast intention in one sentence.
  2. Roast, observe, or study a roast video with the lesson idea in mind.
  3. Cup the result or a comparable coffee using a simple written form.
  4. Brew the same coffee with one normal method and compare it to the cupping result.
  5. Write one next-roast change and one reason not to change anything else.

Common beginner mistakes

  • Trusting the curve before tasting the coffee.
  • Changing charge, airflow, batch size, and development all at once.
  • Using one roast defect word as a verdict without checking green coffee, machine, log, and cup.
  • Ignoring brew behavior after a roast cups well.

Self-check with answer guide

1. What should this lesson change in your roasting judgment?

Answer: It should give you one clearer observation or decision rule, not a memorized number.

2. How do cupping and brewing protect you from false confidence?

Answer: Cupping isolates roast and green coffee signals; brewing tests whether the roast works in the way someone will actually drink it.

3. What is the safest next experiment?

Answer: Repeat the roast with one planned change, keep the same evaluation method, and compare notes side by side.

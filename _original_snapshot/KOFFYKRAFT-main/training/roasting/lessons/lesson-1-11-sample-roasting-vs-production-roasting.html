Sample Roasting vs Production Roasting

The roasting learning loop

  1. Intend: name the coffee, brew use, and flavor goal before heating the machine.
  2. Plan: choose batch size, charge approach, airflow, milestones, and one variable to watch.
  3. Roast and log: record time, temperature, colour, aroma, sound, airflow, gas or power, and decisions.
  4. Cup: evaluate with the Cupping course discipline before defending the profile.
  5. Brew: test with a realistic brew method from the Brewing course.
  6. Revise: change one thing in the next roast and write why.
What this lesson teaches

Sample roasting reveals green coffee potential; production roasting serves a product goal. They are related but not the same job.

How this builds the student

This lesson builds evaluation ethics.

Cupping connection

Use aroma, sweetness, acidity, body, aftertaste, balance, defects, and confidence notes from the Cupping course. A roast decision is not proven until the cup supports it.

Brewing connection

Check whether the roast extracts sensibly in a real recipe. Sour, hollow, bitter, harsh, or muddy brews may point back to roast development, solubility, or brew setup.

From the KoffyKraft notes

Module 1: Introduction to Coffee Roasting

Lesson Objective

By the end of this lesson, you will:

Understand the difference between sample roasting and production roasting,

Learn the purpose and goals of each,

Recognize how roast strategies differ depending on the situation,

Begin planning appropriate roast profiles based on the intended outcome.

  1. What is Sample Roasting?

Sample roasting is roasting small batches of green coffee to evaluate the bean's natural qualities.

Main goal: Reveal origin character (acidity, sweetness, defects, balance),

Not optimized for final drink taste - optimized for bean judgment,

Common in green coffee buying, lot approvals, harvest monitoring.

  1. What is Production Roasting?

Production roasting is roasting coffee to create a finished product for drinking.

Main goal: Craft a delicious, consistent cup for customers or personal use,

Roasts are adjusted to suit brewing methods, flavor preferences, and market needs,

Focus on drinkability, balance, sweetness, texture.

  1. Key Differences Between Sample and Production Roasting
AspectSample RoastingProduction Roasting
PurposeBean evaluationBeverage creation
Batch SizeVery small (50g-500g)Medium to large (2kg-15kg+)
Roast TargetLight, neutral, revealingTargeted to desired flavor goal
End TemperatureLowerVariable (higher for darker profiles)
Curve ControlPriority on evenness, clarityPriority on body, sweetness, balance
EquipmentSmall sample roasters or controlled small-batch setupsLarger production roasters designed for repeatable commercial batches
  1. How Roast Profiles Differ
Roast PhaseSample Roast BehaviorProduction Roast Behavior
Drying PhaseModerate to quick (prevent bean damage)Controlled according to batch size and density
Maillard PhaseShort to moderate (clarity over body)Can be extended for body and flavor depth
Development PhaseShort (preserve acids, show defects)Tailored - short for brightness, long for bittersweetness
  1. Why Not Use a Sample Profile for Production?

Sample roasts show what the bean can offer - but not how to maximize that in the cup.

Production roasts adjust:

Roast depth,

Maillard caramelization,

Development time,

Body building,

Bitterness control,

Different goals mean different curve shapes and decisions.

  1. Practical Beginner Exercise

Option A - Compare Commercial Roasts:

Buy a light roast and a medium/dark roast from specialty cafes.

Observe:

Acidity level?

Body?

Sweetness?

Bitterness?

Guess: Was the roast closer to sample or production style?

Option B - Draft a Mini Roast Plan:

If asked to sample roast a new green coffee:

Charge Temperature: Medium (~190 deg C drum temp),

Fast but controlled Drying (3-4 min),

Shorter Maillard (preserve clarity),

Short Development (bright, clean finish).

  1. Key Takeaways

Sample roasting reveals bean potential.

Production roasting shapes the final drinking experience.

Roast curves, phase timing, and goals differ between sample and production.

Skillful roasters adjust depending on purpose - not every roast is treated the same way.

Reflection Prompt

If you sample roast a bean and find it bright but thin,

how would you adjust your production roast to improve balance?

(Think about Maillard phase and Development time.)

End of Lesson 1.11

Practice assignment

  1. State the roast intention in one sentence.
  2. Roast, observe, or study a roast video with the lesson idea in mind.
  3. Cup the result or a comparable coffee using a simple written form.
  4. Brew the same coffee with one normal method and compare it to the cupping result.
  5. Write one next-roast change and one reason not to change anything else.

Common beginner mistakes

  • Trusting the curve before tasting the coffee.
  • Changing charge, airflow, batch size, and development all at once.
  • Using one roast defect word as a verdict without checking green coffee, machine, log, and cup.
  • Ignoring brew behavior after a roast cups well.

Self-check with answer guide

1. What should this lesson change in your roasting judgment?

Answer: It should give you one clearer observation or decision rule, not a memorized number.

2. How do cupping and brewing protect you from false confidence?

Answer: Cupping isolates roast and green coffee signals; brewing tests whether the roast works in the way someone will actually drink it.

3. What is the safest next experiment?

Answer: Repeat the roast with one planned change, keep the same evaluation method, and compare notes side by side.

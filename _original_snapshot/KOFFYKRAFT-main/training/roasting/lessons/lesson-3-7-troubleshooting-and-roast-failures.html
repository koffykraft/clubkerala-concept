Production and Quality / Lesson 3.7

Troubleshooting & Roast Failures

The roasting learning loop

  1. Intend: name the coffee, brew use, and flavor goal before heating the machine.
  2. Plan: choose batch size, charge approach, airflow, milestones, and one variable to watch.
  3. Roast and log: record time, temperature, colour, aroma, sound, airflow, gas or power, and decisions.
  4. Cup: evaluate with the Cupping course discipline before defending the profile.
  5. Brew: test with a realistic brew method from the Brewing course.
  6. Revise: change one thing in the next roast and write why.
What this lesson teaches

Roast problems are solved by sequence: define symptom, check green coffee, check machine, check profile, cup, then change one variable.

How this builds the student

This lesson builds professional calm under uncertainty.

Cupping connection

Use aroma, sweetness, acidity, body, aftertaste, balance, defects, and confidence notes from the Cupping course. A roast decision is not proven until the cup supports it.

Brewing connection

Check whether the roast extracts sensibly in a real recipe. Sour, hollow, bitter, harsh, or muddy brews may point back to roast development, solubility, or brew setup.

From the KoffyKraft notes

Learning Goal

Learn to identify, diagnose, and resolve common roast defects using flavor cues, curve analysis, and log review - building confidence in problem-solving.

A. Why Troubleshooting Matters

  • Fixing mistakes strengthens learning- Avoids repeated quality loss- Equips you to respond to unexpected changes- Improves feedback from team or customers

B. Common Roast Failures

DefectDescriptionLikely Cause
UnderdevelopedGrassy, sour, no sweetnessToo short dev, low RoR
BakedFlat, dull, cardboard-likeRoR crash, long roast, stalled dev
ScorchedBurnt edge, dry bitternessExcessive charge temp, fast gas
TippedAshy, harsh, localized dark spotsUneven heat, slow bean movement
FlickSpike in RoR post-crack, uneven finishDelayed airflow, late gas cut
CrashSudden RoR dip after crackOver-adjustment, low thermal energy

C. Diagnosis Table

Symptom in CupRoast Log ClueSuggested Fix
Sour + thin bodyShort dev time (<12%)Extend dev, smooth RoR post-crack
Flat + muted flavorRoR crash after 1st crackIncrease airflow or drop slightly earlier
Bitter edge, dark tipHigh charge + early temp spikeLower charge or reduce gas early
Uneven flavor per cupFlick post-crackSoften flame cut, adjust fan timing
Papery, dull cupExcessively long roast (15+ mins)Shorten roast or raise Maillard heat

D. Root Cause Flowchart

Was First Crack Normal?- If early (<7 min): too aggressive or high charge- If late (>10 min): cold drum or low chargeWas RoR declining smoothly?- If not: check airflow, gas ramp, fan timingWas development short or excessive?- <12% = likely sour- >28% = muted, dry finishFlick or Crash?- Flick = sudden RoR spike post-crack (reduce gas more gradually)- Crash = RoR drops flat or dips (maintain post-crack heat energy)

E. Exercise - Simulate a Roast Diagnosis

  1. Review a past roast that tasted off2. Write down:- Time to yellowing- First crack timing- Total time- Drop temp and dev time- Flavor notes: What was wrong?3. Hypothesize the likely issue4. Propose a fix5. Try again - and record everything

F. Analogy - Like Diagnosing a Bad Recipe

  • Roasting is like cooking - a dull curry or bitter stew can result from wrong heat, time, or balance- The trick is to taste + trace the mistake to a roast phase, then adjust just that

H. Sustainability Note

  • Troubleshooting reduces cumulative waste- Preserves green coffee quality and value- Empowers confident roasting across origins

I. Sample Reflection Prompt

Prompt: "This roast looked perfect, but the cup was flat and dull."Answer: Likely a crash or overextended dev. Try shorter roast or higher Maillard energy next time. Always check RoR stability post-crack.

Practice assignment

  1. State the roast intention in one sentence.
  2. Roast, observe, or study a roast video with the lesson idea in mind.
  3. Cup the result or a comparable coffee using a simple written form.
  4. Brew the same coffee with one normal method and compare it to the cupping result.
  5. Write one next-roast change and one reason not to change anything else.

Common beginner mistakes

  • Trusting the curve before tasting the coffee.
  • Changing charge, airflow, batch size, and development all at once.
  • Using one roast defect word as a verdict without checking green coffee, machine, log, and cup.
  • Ignoring brew behavior after a roast cups well.

Self-check with answer guide

1. What should this lesson change in your roasting judgment?

Answer: It should give you one clearer observation or decision rule, not a memorized number.

2. How do cupping and brewing protect you from false confidence?

Answer: Cupping isolates roast and green coffee signals; brewing tests whether the roast works in the way someone will actually drink it.

3. What is the safest next experiment?

Answer: Repeat the roast with one planned change, keep the same evaluation method, and compare notes side by side.

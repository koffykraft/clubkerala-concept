Preparing for Roasting (Indian Coffee Focus)

The roasting learning loop

  1. Intend: name the coffee, brew use, and flavor goal before heating the machine.
  2. Plan: choose batch size, charge approach, airflow, milestones, and one variable to watch.
  3. Roast and log: record time, temperature, colour, aroma, sound, airflow, gas or power, and decisions.
  4. Cup: evaluate with the Cupping course discipline before defending the profile.
  5. Brew: test with a realistic brew method from the Brewing course.
  6. Revise: change one thing in the next roast and write why.
What this lesson teaches

Indian coffees require specific attention to species, process, monsooning, density, moisture, age, and desired brew style.

How this builds the student

This lesson builds local origin judgment.

Cupping connection

Use aroma, sweetness, acidity, body, aftertaste, balance, defects, and confidence notes from the Cupping course. A roast decision is not proven until the cup supports it.

Brewing connection

Check whether the roast extracts sensibly in a real recipe. Sour, hollow, bitter, harsh, or muddy brews may point back to roast development, solubility, or brew setup.

From the KoffyKraft notes

Module 1: Introduction to Coffee Roasting

Lesson Objective

By the end of this lesson, you will:

Begin building core roasting skills, even without a roasting machine,

Understand specific behaviors and strategies for roasting Indian coffees,

Prepare yourself to make better decisions when you start roasting.

  1. Building Roasting Skills Without a Machine

You do not need equipment to start learning how roasting works.

Key skills you can build right now:

Observation: Watch roasting videos, observe color, timing, and stages.

Planning: Practice drafting roast plans based on bean characteristics and flavor goals.

Tasting: Taste different coffees, compare roast levels, link flavor and structure.

Curve Analysis: Study sample roast curves, understand Rate of Rise behavior.

  1. Watching Roasting Videos (Practical Tips)

Focus on the key transitions:

Turning Point,

Dry End (yellowing),

First Crack,

Drop.

Listen for First Crack - not all videos record it clearly, so observe bean color changes too.

Sketch basic curves if possible (no need for precision - purpose is learning flow).

  1. Creating Simple Roast Plans

Pick a coffee type (example: Plantation A Arabica from Chikmagalur).

Write a simple plan:

PhaseTarget
Drying Phase3.5-4 minutes
Maillard Phase4-5 minutes
Development Phase1.5-2 minutes
End Temp~200-202 deg C bean temperature (for medium-light finish)

Adjust depending on whether you want higher brightness or deeper body.

  1. Tasting Practice for Beginners

Find and taste different roast levels of Indian coffees:

Washed Arabica (light roast and medium roast),

Natural process Arabica,

Robusta (medium-dark roast),

Monsooned Malabar (special process).

Observe:

Acidity: High, medium, low?

Sweetness: Bright (fruity) or deep (caramel)?

Body: Light, medium, heavy?

Aftertaste: Clean, dry, lingering?

Record simple notes - start building your tasting language.

  1. Understanding Indian Coffees for Roasting
FactorTypical Indian Coffee BehaviorRoasting Implication
DensityMedium to medium-low compared to AfricansHeat management must be moderate; tipping risk if too aggressive early
Moisture ContentGenerally 10-11%, but naturals and monsooned vary widelyNaturals need longer drying; monsooned needs special slow approaches
Flavor ProfileChocolate, nuts, spices, low floral notes in most lotsDevelopment phase tuning critical to avoid flatness
Processing TypeWashed, natural, monsoonedEach needs different airflow and drying attention

Key Practical Tips:

Washed Arabicas: Use moderate charge temp; preserve clarity with smooth RoR.

Natural Arabicas: Control early heat; gentle Maillard to avoid roughness.

Monsooned Malabar: Slow, steady heat; long Maillard and Development; monitor for easy burning.

  1. Practical Exercise

Option A - Watch and Analyze:

Watch 2-3 roasting videos (light roast, medium roast).

Sketch:

How long was Drying?

How long was Maillard?

How soon after First Crack did they drop?

Option B - Taste and Compare:

Taste a medium roast washed Arabica vs a natural.

Note:

Which had more acidity?

Which had heavier body?

What could the roasting approach have been?

  1. Key Takeaways

Skill development starts with observation, planning, and tasting, not equipment.

Indian coffees behave differently - learn to manage heat and timing carefully.

Building early habits helps transition easily into practical roasting later.

End of New Lesson 1.12

Practice assignment

  1. State the roast intention in one sentence.
  2. Roast, observe, or study a roast video with the lesson idea in mind.
  3. Cup the result or a comparable coffee using a simple written form.
  4. Brew the same coffee with one normal method and compare it to the cupping result.
  5. Write one next-roast change and one reason not to change anything else.

Common beginner mistakes

  • Trusting the curve before tasting the coffee.
  • Changing charge, airflow, batch size, and development all at once.
  • Using one roast defect word as a verdict without checking green coffee, machine, log, and cup.
  • Ignoring brew behavior after a roast cups well.

Self-check with answer guide

1. What should this lesson change in your roasting judgment?

Answer: It should give you one clearer observation or decision rule, not a memorized number.

2. How do cupping and brewing protect you from false confidence?

Answer: Cupping isolates roast and green coffee signals; brewing tests whether the roast works in the way someone will actually drink it.

3. What is the safest next experiment?

Answer: Repeat the roast with one planned change, keep the same evaluation method, and compare notes side by side.

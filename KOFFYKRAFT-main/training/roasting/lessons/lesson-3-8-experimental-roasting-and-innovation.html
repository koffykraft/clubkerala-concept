Production and Quality / Lesson 3.8

Experimental Roasting & Innovation

The roasting learning loop

  1. Intend: name the coffee, brew use, and flavor goal before heating the machine.
  2. Plan: choose batch size, charge approach, airflow, milestones, and one variable to watch.
  3. Roast and log: record time, temperature, colour, aroma, sound, airflow, gas or power, and decisions.
  4. Cup: evaluate with the Cupping course discipline before defending the profile.
  5. Brew: test with a realistic brew method from the Brewing course.
  6. Revise: change one thing in the next roast and write why.
What this lesson teaches

Experimental roasting needs a question, a control, a variable, a record, and a tasting protocol.

How this builds the student

This lesson builds innovation without chaos.

Cupping connection

Use aroma, sweetness, acidity, body, aftertaste, balance, defects, and confidence notes from the Cupping course. A roast decision is not proven until the cup supports it.

Brewing connection

Check whether the roast extracts sensibly in a real recipe. Sour, hollow, bitter, harsh, or muddy brews may point back to roast development, solubility, or brew setup.

From the KoffyKraft notes

Learning Goal

Explore creative and methodical approaches to experiment with roast curves, airflow, and origin characteristics - fostering innovation while staying grounded in sensory feedback.

A. Why Experimentation Matters

  • Pushes your learning beyond repetition- Helps adapt profiles to new beans or customer preferences- Sparks product development (e.g., cold brew, espresso blends)- Encourages curiosity while staying grounded in data

B. Safe Zones for Experimentation

ElementWhat to Try
Charge TempVary +/-5 deg C to shift drying behavior
Maillard TimeShorten or extend to alter body/sweetness
Post-Crack DevExplore 15% vs 30% to adjust clarity
Airflow StrategyHigh pre-crack vs high post-crack
Roast DurationTry 8-min vs 11-min profiles on same bean

C. How to Design a Roast Experiment

1. Choose one variable (e.g., longer Maillard)2. Keep all other settings stable3. Roast 2-3 small batches4. Cup blind with a friend or colleague5. Record scores for acidity, body, aftertaste, balance6. Note preferred batch and flavor description7. Optional: Freeze samples for later retesting

D. Example Experiments to Try

  • Split a washed Ethiopian into: a) Slow 12-min roast with 27% dev b) Fast 9-min roast with 18% dev-> Cup and compare: Is clarity or sweetness better?- Use the same Colombian roast but increase post-crack airflow-> Does it clean the cup or thin the body?

E. Innovation Traps to Avoid

  • Changing too many variables at once- Not documenting or cupping results- Relying only on color or numbers - taste always decides- Ignoring customer brew methods (filter != espresso!)

F. Analogy - Like Jazz Improvisation

  • Start with structure (key, rhythm), then play creatively- Know your baseline roast - then improvise 1 variable at a time- Let the coffee "speak" through change, not noise

G. Quiz (Self-Scoring)

1. What's a safe way to test airflow effect? a) Change airflow and gas at once b) Increase airflow post-crack only c) Switch to a different bean2. Which is NOT a good experiment? a) Faster Maillard b) 3 variables changed c) DTR variation3. True or False: You should always cup blind when testing4. Changing only 1 thing helps you: a) Roast faster b) Reduce electricity c) Understand impact clearly5. Why experiment at all? a) Boredom b) Better flavor insight c) Impress friendsAnswers: 1=b, 2=b, 3=True, 4=c, 5=b

H. Sustainability Note

  • Experimentation helps adapt to new harvests- Supports better use of high-quality lots across different roast goals- Reduces waste from trial-and-error by applying thoughtful design

I. Sample Reflection Prompt

Prompt: "My faster roast had more acidity but lacked depth."Answer: Consider extending Maillard slightly or slowing the post-crack phase. Acidity was preserved - now refine sweetness and body.

Practice assignment

  1. State the roast intention in one sentence.
  2. Roast, observe, or study a roast video with the lesson idea in mind.
  3. Cup the result or a comparable coffee using a simple written form.
  4. Brew the same coffee with one normal method and compare it to the cupping result.
  5. Write one next-roast change and one reason not to change anything else.

Common beginner mistakes

  • Trusting the curve before tasting the coffee.
  • Changing charge, airflow, batch size, and development all at once.
  • Using one roast defect word as a verdict without checking green coffee, machine, log, and cup.
  • Ignoring brew behavior after a roast cups well.

Self-check with answer guide

1. What should this lesson change in your roasting judgment?

Answer: It should give you one clearer observation or decision rule, not a memorized number.

2. How do cupping and brewing protect you from false confidence?

Answer: Cupping isolates roast and green coffee signals; brewing tests whether the roast works in the way someone will actually drink it.

3. What is the safest next experiment?

Answer: Repeat the roast with one planned change, keep the same evaluation method, and compare notes side by side.

Before Module 2: How to Combine What You Feel - A Beginner's Compass

How to use this: Read once before a session, then return only to the checklist you need. The goal is calmer practice, not more pressure.

If you're here, you've already begun noticing individual parts of a cup - sweetness, acidity, body, aroma. Now, in Module 2, we move from noticing single things to understanding how they come together. This shift can feel overwhelming. This guide is written to help you feel grounded, focused, and ready. There is no rush. Your palate, like a compass, will become more steady with use.

Why This Shift Matters

In Module 1, you learned how to observe sensory elements one by one. Now, you're asked to evaluate them as a whole. You're no longer just tasting sweetness - you're asking: is it balanced? does it support or distract? This integration is key to becoming fluent in flavor.

What Is Balance?

Balance is when no single sensation overwhelms the others. A balanced cup might be bright, but not sharp. Sweet, but not flat. Full-bodied, but not muddy. Balance doesn't mean bland. It means harmony. In Module 2, we train your eye (and tongue) to see this harmony.

What Is a Defect?

A defect is a sensory fault - something unpleasant, distracting, or unnatural. But detecting defects is not about judgment. It's about clarity. Moldy beans, over-fermentation, or improper storage can cause sourness, mustiness, or bitterness. These don't just ruin flavor - they mask the coffee's true character. You'll learn to notice defects with clarity, not fear.

What Is Consistency?

Consistency means repeatability. Can you describe a coffee the same way, three days in a row? Can others taste what you do? This is not about being 'right'. It's about training your attention and building memory. The more consistent you are with yourself, the more confident you'll feel.

Learning to Trust What You Sense

Many beginners feel unsure. 'Am I imagining this flavor?' 'What if I'm wrong?' Let go of the idea of right or wrong. You are not being tested - you are learning a language. You don't need to get the 'correct' word. You only need to describe what you honestly feel. Over time, that language sharpens.

Building Your Sensory Vocabulary

  • Write every aroma or flavor that comes to mind. Use your own words first (e.g., 'like my grandma's kitchen').
  • Later, try matching to known categories (floral, spicy, fruity, nutty).
  • Read other people's notes. Don't copy - compare.
  • Notice what repeats. Patterns are the roots of memory.

Tools That Help

  • A notebook or cupping journal - for tasting notes, reflections, and adjustments.
  • A calendar - mark which coffees you cup and when.
  • Your own words - trust them, use them, refine them slowly.

Practice: Your First Integrated Tasting

  1. Choose one coffee. Cup it as you did in Module 1.
  2. After aroma, acidity, sweetness, and body - pause.
  3. Ask: How did these parts fit together? What stood out? What didn't?
  4. Write 3 sentences, not just attributes. e.g., 'This was a bright, lively cup. The acidity was clear, but the body felt missing.'

Reflection: Internal Check-In

  • What felt different when trying to evaluate the whole cup, not just a part?
  • Did you hesitate to describe something because you weren't sure?
  • What might help you feel more ready for comparative and consistency-focused lessons?

Carry this into your next cupping

  • Prepare the space before preparing the coffee.
  • Use fewer cups if you feel overloaded.
  • Write what you notice before checking what others say.
  • Repeat one exercise twice before judging progress.

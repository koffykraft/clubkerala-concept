Roasting for Flavor Goals

The roasting learning loop

  1. Intend: name the coffee, brew use, and flavor goal before heating the machine.
  2. Plan: choose batch size, charge approach, airflow, milestones, and one variable to watch.
  3. Roast and log: record time, temperature, colour, aroma, sound, airflow, gas or power, and decisions.
  4. Cup: evaluate with the Cupping course discipline before defending the profile.
  5. Brew: test with a realistic brew method from the Brewing course.
  6. Revise: change one thing in the next roast and write why.
What this lesson teaches

Roasting for flavor means setting an intention before the roast and checking that intention through cupping and brewing.

How this builds the student

This lesson builds goal-led profile design.

Cupping connection

Use aroma, sweetness, acidity, body, aftertaste, balance, defects, and confidence notes from the Cupping course. A roast decision is not proven until the cup supports it.

Brewing connection

Check whether the roast extracts sensibly in a real recipe. Sour, hollow, bitter, harsh, or muddy brews may point back to roast development, solubility, or brew setup.

From the KoffyKraft notes

Module 1: Introduction to Coffee Roasting

Lesson Objective

By the end of this lesson, you will:

Understand how roast design affects the final flavor,

Identify the basic roast stages that influence different taste characteristics,

Plan roasting approaches based on clear flavor goals.

  1. What Are Flavor Goals?

Flavor goals are the taste and texture outcomes you want to highlight in a coffee.

Common goals include:

Brightness (high acidity, lively flavor),

Sweetness (caramelization, balance),

Body (richness, thickness),

Bittersweetness (structured dark notes).

Choosing a flavor goal before roasting helps you manage heat and timing to suit the bean's natural qualities.

  1. Roast Phases and Flavor Impact
PhaseShorter/FasterLonger/Slower
Drying PhasePreserves acidity, livelinessSoftens acidity, smooths flavor
Maillard Phase (Browning)Lighter sweetness, floral/fruity notesDeeper sweetness, caramel, nuts
Development PhaseHighlights origin clarityLowers acidity, adds body and cocoa notes

Each phase influences flavor differently.

Adjusting the length and energy in each phase allows the roaster to guide the final taste.

  1. Setting Flavor Goals and Basic Roast Plans
Flavor GoalRoast Plan Overview
BrightnessModerate charge, fast Drying, shorter Maillard, shorter Development
SweetnessModerate pace through Maillard, balanced Development (~20-24% of total time)
BodyHigher charge, longer Maillard, longer Development (>25% of total roast time)
BittersweetnessLonger Maillard and Development, higher drop temperatures

Note:

Higher DTR (Development Time Ratio) often increases body but can reduce brightness if extended too far.

  1. Basic Adjustments to Influence Flavor
VariableEffect
Higher charge temperatureMore body, faster drying, risk of scorching if mismanaged
Lower charge temperatureSlower start, more control over Maillard reactions
Longer Maillard phaseMore caramelized sweetness and body
Shorter Maillard phaseHigher acidity, lighter body
Longer DevelopmentLower acidity, deeper body, risk of flatness if overextended
Shorter DevelopmentHigh clarity, but risk of underdeveloped flavors
  1. Practical Beginner Example

Goal: Bright, clean coffee (e.g., washed Ethiopian for pour-over).

Plan:

Moderate charge (not too high),

Fast, but controlled Drying (~3-4 minutes),

Shorter Maillard (~3-4 minutes),

Short Development (~1.5-2 minutes after First Crack),

Drop before heavy caramelization (light-medium roast).

  1. Key Takeaways

Flavor goals help structure roasting choices.

Each roast phase affects different flavor traits.

Heat management and timing adjustments are the basic tools for influencing the cup.

Planning with a goal reduces guesswork and improves consistency.

End of Lesson 1.10

Practice assignment

  1. State the roast intention in one sentence.
  2. Roast, observe, or study a roast video with the lesson idea in mind.
  3. Cup the result or a comparable coffee using a simple written form.
  4. Brew the same coffee with one normal method and compare it to the cupping result.
  5. Write one next-roast change and one reason not to change anything else.

Common beginner mistakes

  • Trusting the curve before tasting the coffee.
  • Changing charge, airflow, batch size, and development all at once.
  • Using one roast defect word as a verdict without checking green coffee, machine, log, and cup.
  • Ignoring brew behavior after a roast cups well.

Self-check with answer guide

1. What should this lesson change in your roasting judgment?

Answer: It should give you one clearer observation or decision rule, not a memorized number.

2. How do cupping and brewing protect you from false confidence?

Answer: Cupping isolates roast and green coffee signals; brewing tests whether the roast works in the way someone will actually drink it.

3. What is the safest next experiment?

Answer: Repeat the roast with one planned change, keep the same evaluation method, and compare notes side by side.

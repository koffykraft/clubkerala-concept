Applied Control / Lesson 2.3

Cupping & Sensory Alignment

The roasting learning loop

  1. Intend: name the coffee, brew use, and flavor goal before heating the machine.
  2. Plan: choose batch size, charge approach, airflow, milestones, and one variable to watch.
  3. Roast and log: record time, temperature, colour, aroma, sound, airflow, gas or power, and decisions.
  4. Cup: evaluate with the Cupping course discipline before defending the profile.
  5. Brew: test with a realistic brew method from the Brewing course.
  6. Revise: change one thing in the next roast and write why.
What this lesson teaches

Roasters need cupping because roasting decisions must be evaluated in the cup with shared language and repeatable method.

How this builds the student

This lesson connects roasting to the Cupping course.

Cupping connection

Use aroma, sweetness, acidity, body, aftertaste, balance, defects, and confidence notes from the Cupping course. A roast decision is not proven until the cup supports it.

Brewing connection

Check whether the roast extracts sensibly in a real recipe. Sour, hollow, bitter, harsh, or muddy brews may point back to roast development, solubility, or brew setup.

From the KoffyKraft notes

Learning Goal

Learn how to cup coffee professionally, how to build your sensory vocabulary, and how to align your perception with industry standards - even if you're cupping in a small space or as a beginner.

A. Why Cupping Matters

Cupping is the universal language of coffee quality.

It allows you to:

Evaluate a roast objectively

Compare different coffees fairly

Track changes during development, aging, blending

Communicate results with farmers, buyers, baristas

It's essential for:

Roasters, Q Graders, cafe owners, trainers, and buyers

B. Cupping vs Tasting

PracticeDescriptionPurpose
CuppingStandardized method with bowls, spoonsEvaluate roast quality and defects
TastingInformal sipping of brewed coffeeEnjoyment, general preference

Cupping is repeatable, comparable, and scored - unlike casual tasting.

C. Cupping Protocol (SCA Style)

StepDetails
Coffee dose8.25g +/- 0.25g per 150 ml water
Grind sizeMedium-coarse, similar to French press
Water temp93 deg C +/- 1 deg C (off boil, not too hot)
Brew time4 minutes steep, then break crust
Slurp & evaluateAt 4-12 minutes, once cool enough
Score & recordAcidity, sweetness, body, aftertaste, uniformity, defects

Use the SCA cupping form or a simplified sensory map for beginners.

D. Sensory Categories to Track

AttributeDescriptionExamples
AromaSmell before and after adding waterFloral, nutty, earthy
AcidityBrightness or livelinessCitric (orange), malic (apple), tartaric (grape)
SweetnessNatural sugar presenceHoney, caramel, fruit sugars
BodyMouthfeel or textureCreamy, watery, oily
AftertasteFlavor lingering after swallowingLong, short, clean, bitter
BalanceHarmony of all attributesEven integration of notes

E. Aligning Your Palate with the Industry

Use reference flavors (e.g., lemon juice, black tea, raw cocoa, nuts)

Cup with others if possible - discuss and compare

Record using grids - build patterns over time

Use a public flavor wheel or a simple flavor triangle: brightness, sweetness, and body.

F. Real-World Exercise (No Lab Needed)

Option 1 - Solo Cupping at Home:

Use 2-3 coffees (light, medium, dark)

Label cups A, B, C

Use a timer, scales, spoon

Focus on: aroma, acidity, sweetness, body, aftertaste

Option 2 - Triangle Test (Sensory Alignment):

Brew 2 cups of Coffee A, 1 cup of Coffee B

Mix and taste blindly - can you identify the odd one out?

Helps calibrate palate sensitivity

G. Beginner Analogy: Wine Flight vs Judging

Casual wine tasting = sipping for pleasure

Wine judging = controlled, comparative, repeatable

Cupping is the coffee version of judging - done to understand, not just enjoy.

H. Quiz (Self-Scoring)

What is the main purpose of cupping?

a) Serving coffee b) Quality assessment c) Brewing at home

True or False: Tasting and cupping are identical.

What attribute refers to mouthfeel?

a) Body b) Aroma c) Acidity

a) Milk b) Orange c) Cocoa

In triangle cupping, how many cups do you compare?

a) 2 b) 3 c) 4

Answers: 1=b, 2=False, 3=a, 4=b, 5=b

I. Sustainability Note

Proper cupping = less waste, better decision-making

It ensures coffee is valued fairly, especially from small producers

A calibrated cupper = honest buyer = farmer gets deserved price

J. Sample Reflection Prompt

"My coffee had great aroma and body but tasted flat. What could this mean?"

Sample Answer:

The roast may have muted acidity through overdevelopment. Try a lighter roast or shorter development time to preserve brightness.

K. Glossary

Cupping: Standardized method for evaluating coffee flavor

Acidity: Bright, tart quality in coffee (not sourness)

Body: Mouthfeel - light, heavy, silky, or thin

Triangle Test: Three-cup comparison to find the odd sample

SCA: Specialty Coffee Association - sets industry cupping protocol

L. Further Study

Use the course reference page, a public cupping form, and your own repeated tasting notes to calibrate vocabulary. Prioritize repeatability over clever descriptions.

Practice assignment

  1. State the roast intention in one sentence.
  2. Roast, observe, or study a roast video with the lesson idea in mind.
  3. Cup the result or a comparable coffee using a simple written form.
  4. Brew the same coffee with one normal method and compare it to the cupping result.
  5. Write one next-roast change and one reason not to change anything else.

Common beginner mistakes

  • Trusting the curve before tasting the coffee.
  • Changing charge, airflow, batch size, and development all at once.
  • Using one roast defect word as a verdict without checking green coffee, machine, log, and cup.
  • Ignoring brew behavior after a roast cups well.

Self-check with answer guide

1. What should this lesson change in your roasting judgment?

Answer: It should give you one clearer observation or decision rule, not a memorized number.

2. How do cupping and brewing protect you from false confidence?

Answer: Cupping isolates roast and green coffee signals; brewing tests whether the roast works in the way someone will actually drink it.

3. What is the safest next experiment?

Answer: Repeat the roast with one planned change, keep the same evaluation method, and compare notes side by side.
